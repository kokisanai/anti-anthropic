# Anti-Anthropic

## Core Lens

Treat Anthropic as a market-education machine, not merely an enemy. The stronger a model platform becomes, the more it proves demand, embeds itself into workflows, and exposes control points that other companies can monetize.

Use "anti" as a business posture:

- Use Anthropic as the benchmark.
- Locate where users feel trapped, overcharged, over-governed, under-served, or exposed.
- Convert anger into concrete migration cost, operational risk, or procurement demand.
- Build around one painful control point before claiming a platform.

Do not frame the output as generic dislike, moral outrage, or "build a better Claude." The useful question is: where does platform power create a budgeted need for escape routes, buffers, routers, brakes, sandboxes, memory, migration, or professional last-mile products?

## Workflow

1. Identify the triggering event.
   - Product launch, price change, account suspension, refusal pattern, protocol controversy, trademark pressure, API/billing surprise, data-control issue, competitive restriction, or user backlash.

2. Extract the exact angry sentence.
   - Prefer a concrete complaint such as "I pay for Max and still run out," "I do not trust MCP in production," or "client assets cannot leave our environment."
   - Avoid starting from abstractions like "closed," "expensive," or "unsafe."

3. Classify the control point.
   - Client or entry point
   - Pricing, subscription, quota, or billing opacity
   - Model choice or routing
   - Data storage, memory, or portability
   - Account access, suspension, appeals, or vendor continuity
   - Safety policy, over-refusal, or professional workflow mismatch
   - Protocol, tool permission, MCP security, or ecosystem governance
   - Design system, template, asset, or workflow ownership
   - Enterprise permissions, audit logs, compliance, or procurement

4. Test whether anger is real demand.
   - Are users already using ugly workarounds?
   - Is the failure cost high enough to create budget?
   - Is there a credible replacement, fallback, or unbundling path?
   - Would the opportunity survive if Anthropic fixed the immediate bug?

5. Turn the control point into a wedge.
   - Make the first product narrow, testable, and useful.
   - Examples: MCP scanner, billing anomaly alert, BYOK coding client, local-first design workflow, account-risk report, multi-model fallback gateway, portable memory layer, professional vertical copilot.

6. Expand the wedge into a control layer.
   - Accumulate customer-specific workflows, policies, evals, templates, logs, permissions, history, and routing rules.
   - Make users stay because the product improves their work and preserves their control, not because it traps them.

## Opportunity Map

Use these recurring opportunity types when analyzing a case:

- Billing guard: cost monitoring, anomaly detection, budget caps, cache analysis, runaway-loop brakes, per-project/user/agent cost attribution.
- MCP trust layer: scanner, permission audit, gateway, sandbox, certification, enterprise policy dashboard.
- Model-agnostic agent client: BYOK, private deployment, audit trails, reversible code changes, permission tiers, cost controls.
- AI continuity platform: dependency mapping, fallback routing, account-risk scoring, incident drills, terms monitoring, vendor-risk response.
- Professional over-refusal vertical: industry-specific AI for legitimate sensitive work with identity, logging, auditability, permissions, and liability boundaries.
- Independent memory layer: portable context, preferences, project history, design systems, team norms, privacy, correction, forgetting, and cross-model use.
- Open workflow layer: local-first and model-flexible alternatives for design, presentations, video, websites, reports, knowledge sites, ads, and brand content.
- Enterprise AI control plane: unified APIs, routing, prompt/version management, evals, data redaction, tool permissions, quota controls, chargeback, compliance.

## Output Pattern

When asked to analyze an opportunity, produce:

1. **Market signal**: the visible user pain or controversy.
2. **Hidden control point**: what Anthropic-like platform power is constraining.
3. **Budget owner**: who pays and why now.
4. **First wedge**: the smallest useful product.
5. **Moat path**: what accumulates over time.
6. **Anti-hype check**: why this is more than social-media anger.
7. **Positioning line**: a plain one-sentence promise to users.

Keep the tone strategic, founder-oriented, and concrete. The best opportunity is not where anger is loudest; it is where, after the anger, users reach for their wallets.
